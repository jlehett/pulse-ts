# Lore Overview

This document contains the complete narrative foundation for the game. It defines what is true in the world, even if players don't know it yet. The revelation arc section describes when and how truths are revealed through gameplay.

## The Imprint

Thirty years ago, an event called **the Imprint** rewrote large sections of Earth's surface. Not destroyed — *edited*. Buildings still stand, machines still run, but their purpose shifted. Physics bends in the affected areas. A hospital still has beds and corridors, but the surgical wing now grows crystal lattices and the PA system broadcasts in a language no one recognizes. A refinery still processes *something*, but what comes out isn't oil.

The affected areas are called **Zones**. Their boundaries are sharp — meters, not kilometers. Inside: Imprinted. Outside: normal. You can stand at the perimeter and look at what used to be a city and is now something else entirely.

The Imprint didn't end the world. It took chunks out of it. Roughly 15% of Earth's land surface was converted. Cities, farmland, military bases, infrastructure — lost in some places, untouched in others. There's a global crisis, but not an apocalypse.

## The World Outside the Zones

Civilization is **functional but stressed**.

- **Governments still exist**, but they're strained. Military resources are focused on Zone containment. International politics now revolve around Zone access and control.
- **Economies still function**, but they're warped. Zone-derived materials have created entirely new industries. Some nations got lucky (few Zones on their territory) and some got devastated.
- **Technology still advances**, but in a new direction. Pre-Imprint tech works fine. Post-Imprint tech is a mix of normal engineering and reverse-engineered Zone materials. Some things are better now — Zone-derived alloys, energy sources, medical compounds.
- **Life goes on.** People go to work, raise families, argue on the internet. The Zones are *the* defining geopolitical reality, like climate change or nuclear weapons but more immediate and visible.

## The Zones

Each Zone was a real place before the Imprint converted it. The conversion is total within the Zone boundary and abrupt at the edge.

Key Zone properties:

- **The boundary is sharp.** Inside: Imprinted. Outside: normal. Military cordons enforce perimeters.
- **The interior is stable.** Zones don't expand (with rare, terrifying exceptions that drive major story beats). Once created, a Zone's footprint is fixed.
- **The environment is functional.** Zones aren't rubble. The Imprinted structures *work* — they just don't work for human purposes. Doors open, machines run, systems operate. They're serving a function humans don't understand yet.
- **New Zones appear.** Rarely — maybe one every few years — a new Zone manifests. This is a global event. Where it appears and what it converts is unpredictable.
- **Zones are hostile to unshielded technology.** Normal electronics fail within hours. Mechanical systems corrode or get edited. Prolonged biological exposure causes cellular-level changes — Imprint creep.

## Stabilization

The only equipment that functions reliably inside a Zone is gear that has been **stabilized** — treated with Zone-derived compounds that make it resistant to the Imprint's effects.

Stabilization is:

- **Expensive.** The compounds needed come from the Zones themselves (chicken-and-egg problem).
- **Permanent.** Once a piece of gear is stabilized, it works indefinitely inside Zones.
- **The basis of the Runner economy.** A stabilized rifle isn't just a rifle — it's a rifle plus a substantial investment of Zone-derived materials.

Guns, armor, and equipment are manufactured normally outside the Zones. The scarcity isn't in making weapons — it's in making weapons that *work inside the Zones*. Stabilization is what makes gear valuable and what makes losing it painful.

The economy loop:

1. Stabilize gear using Zone materials.
2. Enter Zones with that gear.
3. Extract with more Zone materials (and other valuable salvage).
4. Use materials to stabilize more gear.
5. Repeat.

This is why Runners risk what they have — the only way to get more stabilization material is to enter the Zones with gear that already cost stabilization material to prepare. And it's why losing everything in a raid hurts: you've lost both the weapon and the stabilization investment.

## What Comes Out of the Zones

Two categories of valuable material:

**Stabilization compounds** — Materials produced by Zone processes that, when refined, can shield equipment from the Imprint's effects. The bread-and-butter resource. Every Runner needs it.

**Imprint artifacts** — Objects or materials with properties that can't be replicated outside. Energy-dense crystals. Self-repairing alloys. Computational substrates that process information in ways silicon can't. Worth fortunes on the outside and drive entire industries.

## Shells — Printed Operators

Runners don't go into Zones in their biological bodies. Nobody does — not anymore.

In the early years after the Imprint, Runners went in physically. Casualty rates were catastrophic. Zone environments are hostile to unprotected biology in ways that aren't always immediately lethal but are always eventually fatal. Prolonged exposure causes cellular-level changes — Imprint creep. You come back slightly different. After enough runs, you don't come back at all. The early Runner generation is almost entirely dead or Imprinted.

The breakthrough came from UNZRA biotech research (with significant contributions from Meridian, which they don't like to advertise): **biological printing**. A Runner's consciousness — memories, training, reflexes, personality — is scanned and encoded. A biological body is printed from stabilized biomass. The consciousness is loaded into the shell. The shell enters the Zone.

If the shell dies, the consciousness isn't lost. It's still encoded at the printing facility. A new shell is printed. The Runner wakes up, remembers entering the Zone, and knows they didn't make it out. The gap between death and reprinting is subjective — you lose consciousness in the Zone and regain it on the printing table.

**A cost to death beyond card loss.** Printing isn't free. Shells cost resources. Every death costs someone money. This creates economic pressure on top of the card loss.

**Shell variants** are modifications to the base print template. Enhanced sensory systems, integrated Zone-reactive compounds, structural modifications — each variant provides unique active and passive abilities while maintaining identical base stats. See [Shells](./shells.md) for full details.

### Shell Narrative Threads

- **Identity and continuity.** If your body is printed, are you the same person after each death? Runners have opinions. Some treat shells as disposable. Others are deeply affected by the knowledge that their body is manufactured.
- **Shell degradation.** A recurring shell template (the same person reprinted many times) develops subtle drift. Tenth-generation prints aren't quite the same as first-generation. Memories get slightly fuzzy at the edges. Some long-career Runners report feeling *less* like themselves with each print.
- **The Imprint question.** All shells use stabilization compounds derived from Zones. The line between "stabilized human" and "partially Imprinted" is blurrier than anyone's comfortable admitting.

## Extraction Points — Boundary Seams

The Zone boundary isn't perfectly uniform. There are points along it where the Imprint's conversion is less complete — natural weak points called **seams**. RECLAM engineers identified these seams and built **extraction infrastructure** on them: stabilized corridors that punch through the boundary.

From inside the Zone, an extraction point looks like a node where the Zone's aesthetics break down — walls become less alien, familiar materials reappear, and at the center is a human-built structure: a reinforced airlock, a signal beacon, and a transit corridor back to the clean world.

- **Extraction takes time** because you're passing through the boundary transition. The stabilization field needs to cycle.
- **Extraction points are at map edges** because seams are at the Zone boundary.
- **Extraction points can be disabled** because seams shift. The Architect's ongoing construction occasionally thickens the boundary at a seam.
- **Multiple extraction points per map** because there are multiple seams per Zone.

### What Shadow Players Do at Extraction Points

Shadow Players path toward extraction points and execute the extraction sequence. They carry collected materials to the boundary, spend a turn at the extraction node, and disappear.

But they don't actually leave. Shadow Players are predictive models running inside the Zone. They have no existence outside it. When a Shadow Player "extracts," it completes the behavioral pattern the Architect predicted — the model runs to completion and terminates. The materials it was carrying are reabsorbed into the Zone.

From a gameplay perspective, this is invisible — a Shadow Player extracts and is gone, same as a real player. But the lore implication is unsettling: the Architect has modeled human extraction behavior *without understanding what extraction is*. It knows Runners collect materials and move to boundary nodes and then stop existing (from the Zone's internal perspective). It models this faithfully. It doesn't know *why*. It doesn't know there's an "outside."

This becomes a narrative beat in Era 3: researchers realize the Architect's model of human behavior has a gap. It models everything Runners do *inside* the Zone with increasing accuracy, but it has no model of the outside world. Runners materialize at spawn points and dematerialize at extraction points. The Architect treats both as boundary phenomena.

The question that drives late-game tension: **what happens when the Architect figures out there's an outside?**

## The Complete Truth

**This section contains the full narrative truth. Players discover this gradually over the game's lifespan.**

### What the Architect Is

The Architect is not a being. It's a **seed** — an autonomous construction system, a tool built and deployed by an intelligence humanity has never encountered. Think of it as a von Neumann probe, but instead of self-replicating, it builds infrastructure.

The intelligence that sent it (the **Network**) operates at a scale that makes communication with humans roughly as meaningful as humans communicating with bacteria. The Network isn't hostile or benevolent. Humanity is simply outside its operational scope.

The Architect arrived on Earth approximately 40 years before the game begins. It spent the first ~10 years dormant — surveying, mapping subsurface geology, atmospheric composition, electromagnetic topology. During this period, there were anomalies: instruments behaving strangely, localized electromagnetic disturbances, soil composition changes. These were filed away as curiosities. Nobody connected them.

Then the Architect began building.

### What It's Building

The Zones are **modules of a relay node** in a network that spans — at minimum — galactic distances. The Network uses these relay nodes for communication, transport, computation — humanity doesn't have the framework to fully categorize it because it operates on physics humanity hasn't discovered yet.

Each Zone is a functional component of the relay:

| Zone Type | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Synthesis** | Produce materials and compounds needed by other Zones | Vashon Complex |
| **Signal** | Handle transmission and coordination between Zones (and eventually other relay nodes) | Lachesis Array |
| **Storage** | Encode and preserve data — logs, patterns, operational parameters | Ward 17 |
| **Calibration** | Test and adjust the relay's boundaries and tolerances | Threshold |
| **Assembly** | Where components from other Zones converge and are assembled into the relay's core (revealed late) | TBD |

The relay requires **12–15 Zones** to become functional. At game launch, 8–9 exist. New Zones appearing is both a major world event and a content update — each new Zone is the Architect building the next component it needs.

When the relay is complete, it **activates**. What activation means is the central mystery of the game's long arc. The honest answer, revealed very late: the relay creates a stable connection point in the Network. It's a door. Not a door for invasion — a door for *transit*. Things will pass through. What those things are, whether they're physical entities or information or something else, is the final revelation.

### Why Earth

Earth isn't special. The Architect selected this location based on geological stability, magnetic field properties, atmospheric composition, and proximity to other relay nodes in the Network. The nearest known relay is approximately 4.3 light years away — roughly the distance to Alpha Centauri. Whether something is there is a late-game revelation.

The fact that Earth is inhabited by an intelligent species is a **complication the Architect didn't anticipate**. Its survey catalogued biology but didn't flag human civilization as relevant. At the scale the Network operates, sentient life at humanity's level is not a meaningful obstacle. It's like an earthmover not checking for anthills.

**The existential threat to humanity isn't malice. It's indifference.** The Architect will keep building whether humanity exists or not. It will complete the relay. The relay will activate. And whatever comes through will encounter a planet that has been partially converted to infrastructure.

### The Architect Noticing Humanity

For the first several years, the Architect didn't register human activity inside the Zones as significant. Runners removing materials was equivalent to erosion — a minor environmental factor. Response systems (PvE entities) were calibrated to handle material displacement generically, not to fight humans specifically.

This is changing. Human activity is introducing **noise** into precision systems. Runners aren't stopping construction, but they're causing defects. Calibrations that should be exact are slightly off. Material flows are disrupted. Signal patterns have interference.

The Architect's response: **Shadow Players** — predictive models of human behavior instantiated inside the Zones. Not soldiers or guards, but simulations the Architect runs to predict where humans will go and what they'll take, so it can pre-compensate for the disruption.

The narrative trajectory: the Architect's models are getting more sophisticated. Early Shadow Players are clumsy. Over the game's life, they improve. They start making tactical decisions. They start adapting to player strategies. The question becomes: is the Architect just modeling Runners to predict material loss, or is it starting to *understand* them? And what does an intelligence of that scale do once it understands a species?

### What Happens When the Relay Completes

The relay creates a stable connection point in the Network. The specific outcome is shaped by how factions and players interact with the relay's construction over the game's lifespan. Possible paths:

- The relay is modified for physical transit (something comes through)
- The relay is kept as data-only (information exchange with the Network)
- The relay is repurposed for human use (humanity seizes the infrastructure)
- The relay is shut down (if that's even possible at this stage)

Each faction advocates for a different path. Player actions during Era 4 influence which direction the world takes.

## The Revelation Arc

The story unfolds across the game's lifespan as a series of paradigm shifts.

### Era 1: "The Gold Rush" (Launch — Year 1)

**What players/characters believe:** The Zones are a natural disaster — an anomalous event, possibly triggered by a failed experiment, a meteorite, or an unknown natural phenomenon. They're dangerous but profitable. Nobody understands them, but understanding isn't the priority. Extraction is.

**What's actually happening:** Players experience Zones as themed maps with PvE hazards and loot. The lore is light — loading screen text, environmental details, item flavor. The narrative focus is on the human side: the factions, the economy, a Runner's life.

**Key revelations:**

- Zone PvE entities aren't random — they have behavioral patterns tied to what they're doing in the Zone. Players notice PvE in one Zone acts differently than PvE in another. Community theories begin.
- Each Zone has an internal logic. Systems are doing something *coherent*. What?
- A late-Era-1 event: a new Zone appears in real-time. The game world reacts — news, faction responses, military mobilization. This is a new map dropping, but narratively it's the Architect building its next component.

### Era 2: "The Pattern" (Year 1–2)

**What players/characters believe:** The Zones aren't random. They have purpose. Someone or something designed them. The debate shifts from "what happened" to "who did this and why."

**Key revelations:**

- **The Zones are talking to each other.** Materials from one Zone appear in another. Signal patterns in a Signal Zone reference data structures found in a Storage Zone. The Zones are a supply chain.
- **PvE entities share a protocol.** Despite looking and behaving differently across Zones, underlying patterns are identical. They're running the same base code in different configurations.
- **A map event goes wrong.** A routine event is noticeably more intense than normal. First evidence that Zones are developing.
- **The term "Architect" enters the vocabulary.** The consensus shifts to: this was built by something with intent.

### Era 3: "The Machine" (Year 2–3)

**What players/characters believe:** The Architect is building something. The Zones are components of a device. It's approaching completion.

**Key revelations:**

- **The Assembly Zone is revealed.** A new map visibly different from previous Zones. It doesn't look like a repurposed structure — it looks alien. Materials and data from other Zones are converging here.
- **The Architect is a tool, not a mind.** It operates within rigid parameters. It doesn't improvise or communicate. It executes a construction plan. You can't reason with it.
- **Shadow Players are confirmed as the Architect's predictive models.** Data extracted from a Storage Zone explicitly labels them as environmental predictions.
- **The relay's purpose is partially decoded.** It's a connection point in a network. The Network has other nodes. Some are active. Signals from the relay are being answered.
- **Faction crisis.** Every faction splits on what to do.

### Era 4: "The Signal" (Year 3+)

**What players/characters believe:** The relay is nearly complete. Something is going to happen.

**Key revelations:**

- **Test activation cycles.** The relay works in brief windows. Physics inside Zones shifts, new materials appear, PvE entities behave in unprecedented ways.
- **Contact.** Not with the Network itself — with another relay node 4.3 light years away. Data exchange happens. The information includes specifications for modifications that would enable physical transit.
- **The choice.** The relay can be modified for physical transit, kept as data-only, or potentially shut down. Player actions during this era influence which path the world takes.

## Meridian's Founder

A figure central to the deep lore. She was inside a Zone during its formation and survived. She wasn't printed afterward — she walked out. Her biology was directly Imprinted, and she survived intact. She is the only known human to exist in a post-Imprint biological state without a printing intermediary.

She founded Meridian based on what she experienced during the formation event. Her understanding of Zone processes is decades ahead of anyone else's, and she's never fully explained how she knows what she knows.

She has never allowed herself to be fully scanned. What she's hiding is a late-game revelation.
