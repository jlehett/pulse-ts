# Narrative Delivery

This document defines how the game's story (see [Lore Overview](./lore-overview.md)) reaches players through gameplay systems. The goal is that players absorb narrative by *playing*, not by reading lore databases — though deeper investigation is rewarded.

## Principles

- **Show, don't tell.** The primary narrative vehicle is what players *see and experience* in raids, not text dumps.
- **Depth is optional, breadth is unavoidable.** Every player encounters surface-level narrative (environmental details, PvE behavior, map identity). Lore-hunters who pursue faction contracts and collect data fragments get the deep story.
- **The community is a narrative layer.** Ambiguity in the early eras should generate community theories. Revelations should confirm some theories and invalidate others.
- **Mechanics are narrative.** Stabilization is a game mechanic AND a world-building element. Shells are a respawn system AND a philosophical question. Shadow Players are AI backfill AND an Architect behavior.

## Environmental Storytelling (Every Raid)

Every PoI has **three readable layers** of environment:

### 1. The Before

What this place was pre-Imprint. Human signs, equipment, personal effects. A hospital had patients. A factory had workers. These traces remain — half-visible under the conversion, recognizable to observant players.

### 2. The Transition

Evidence of the Imprint event itself. The boundary where normal becomes Zone. Half-converted objects. A desk partially restructured into crystalline lattice. A vehicle whose engine was replaced with a biological pump. The seam between what was and what is.

### 3. The Function

What the Zone does *now*. Machinery with no human analogue. Materials flowing through channels that weren't there before. PvE entities executing tasks. This layer is *coherent* — players can watch a Zone operate and start to understand its process, even before lore explains it.

Each PoI should have enough environmental detail that a player who pays attention across multiple raids can piece together: "This used to be X. The Imprint turned it into Y. Now it's doing Z."

## Zone Function as Narrative

Each map is a Zone with a specific function in the Architect's relay construction (see [Lore Overview — What It's Building](./lore-overview.md#what-its-building)). The function isn't labeled — it's expressed through the Zone's environment, PvE behavior, and events.

| Map | Pre-Imprint Location | Zone Function | How Players Read It |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vashon Complex** | Chemical plant | Synthesis (material production) | Machinery is making something. Tanks contain living fluid. Production lines run without operators. |
| **Lachesis Array** | Telecom facility | Signal (inter-Zone coordination) | Dishes point at each other and at the sky. Signals pulse on a schedule. Interference affects detection. |
| **Ward 17** | Hospital campus | Storage (data encoding) | Crystals grow from walls. Patient records have become physical objects. Rooms hum with encoded data. |
| **Threshold** | Mountain research station | Calibration (boundary testing) | The Zone edge is visible from inside. It fluctuates. Equipment measures something at the boundary. |

Cross-Zone connections become apparent in Era 2 when players notice: materials from Vashon look like signal substrates in Lachesis. Data encoded in Ward 17 matches patterns found in Threshold's calibration logs. The Zones are *working together*.

## Loot as Narrative

Items extracted from Zones carry lore through classification and description:

### Stabilization Compounds

Described clinically, with notes on which Zone they originate from and what they were synthesized *for* (from the Architect's perspective). A compound from Vashon might be labeled: "Polymer-class stabilization compound. Zone-origin: Synthesis. Intended application: signal substrate bonding (Architect designation). Human application: equipment stabilization."

### Imprint Artifacts

Described with a mix of scientific analysis and unanswered questions. "This alloy self-repairs at a molecular level. The repair process consumes ambient electromagnetic energy. The mechanism is not understood." These items seed curiosity and community discussion.

### Data Fragments

Rare drops from Storage Zones. Short texts — research logs, intercepted signals, decoded Architect notation — that accumulate into a larger picture. Collected and viewable in a codex. This is the primary vehicle for deep lore delivery.

Data fragments should be:
- **Short.** 2–4 paragraphs max. Dense with implication, light on exposition.
- **Perspectival.** Written from a specific point of view — a researcher's log, a military report, an intercepted signal analysis. Never omniscient narration.
- **Cross-referencing.** Later fragments reference earlier ones. Collecting many fragments reveals connections that any single fragment doesn't show.

### Anomalous Items

Very rare drops that don't fit any category. Items that shouldn't exist. A piece of equipment with human design language but materials that only exist inside Zones. A data fragment written in a human language but containing information no human could know. These seed the deepest mysteries.

## Faction Contracts as Narrative Framing

Before each raid, the player selects a contract (or goes independent). The contract provides:

### Pre-Raid Briefing

Frames the raid from the faction's perspective. Helix says "extract materials from sectors 3 and 7." Wardens say "disrupt the processing node in the Refinery." Same map, different lens. The briefing text is short (2–3 sentences) and establishes the faction's priorities.

### Faction-Specific Objectives

Optional bonus objectives that incentivize engaging with the faction's agenda. These aren't required but reward players who adopt the faction's perspective for the raid.

### Post-Raid Debrief

Interprets what you found through the faction's ideology. You extracted a data fragment. Helix values it at market rate. UNZRA wants to study it. Meridian says it confirms their theory about Zone interdependence. Same item, different meaning. The debrief is 1–2 sentences of faction-colored reaction.

This lets players experience the narrative from multiple perspectives without locking them into one storyline. Running the same map with different faction contracts tells different stories.

## Priority Contracts as Seasonal Story

Each faction's priority contract chain (5–8 raids per season) is the primary narrative progression vehicle. These are designed as a serialized story:

- **Each raid has a specific narrative objective** in a specific Zone, with briefing and debrief text that advances the faction's storyline.
- **Mid-chain twists** change the player's understanding of what they're doing and why.
- **Chain completion unlocks a revelation** — a piece of the larger story that recontextualizes what players have experienced.
- **Cross-faction connections** are visible to players who complete chains for multiple factions. The Wardens' chain and Meridian's chain reference the same events from opposite perspectives.

Priority contract revelations are the mechanism for the era transitions described in the [Lore Overview — Revelation Arc](./lore-overview.md#the-revelation-arc).

## PvE Entities as Narrative

PvE entities are not generic enemies — they're **functional components of the Zone**, and their behavior tells a story.

### Behavior Reflects Zone Function

- **Vashon (Synthesis):** Entities carry materials, feed machines, maintain production lines. They treat everything — including Runners — as raw input.
- **Lachesis (Signal):** Entities broadcast, relay, and amplify. Fighting one might alert entities across the Zone.
- **Ward 17 (Storage):** Entities observe, follow, and crystallize. They're more interested in studying you than stopping you.
- **Threshold (Calibration):** Entities are most aggressive near the Zone edge and passive deep inside. They're protecting the boundary.

Players who pay attention across multiple Zones notice: PvE entities do different things in different Zones, but they feel *related*. Same underlying systems, different configurations. This is the first step toward recognizing the Architect.

### PvE Evolution Across Eras

- **Era 1:** PvE is generic and predictable. Players learn patterns, optimize combat.
- **Era 2:** PvE behavior becomes Zone-specific. Entities in different Zones have distinct behaviors. Community notices.
- **Era 3:** PvE entities begin adapting to Runner behavior. Response systems activate faster. New entity types appear.
- **Era 4:** PvE entities coordinate across PoIs within a Zone. The Zone is acting as a system, not as isolated encounters.

## Shadow Player Evolution as Ambient Narrative

Shadow Players improve over the game's lifespan. This is both a gameplay tuning lever and a narrative beat:

- **Launch:** Shadow Players move predictably. They path toward loot, extract on schedule, fight when cornered. Players learn to identify them quickly.
- **Era 2:** Shadow Players adapt to player density. They avoid high-traffic areas, choose less obvious routes, sometimes set ambush positions. Players begin to doubt: "Was that a real player or a Shadow?"
- **Era 3:** Shadow Players use faction-specific tactics. They sometimes cooperate — two Shadow Players moving in coordination. Players report encounters that felt "too smart."
- **Era 4:** Shadow Players do things players have never seen before. Novel tactics. Unpredictable behavior. The Architect isn't just modeling humans anymore — it's *extrapolating*.

The community's evolving relationship with Shadow Players IS the narrative of the Architect noticing humanity. No cutscene needed.

## Map Events as Zone Expression

Map events (see [Map Events](./map-events.md)) are reframed narratively as the Zone's function expressing itself:

- **Vashon's "Growth Surge"** — the production process ramped up. More valuable drops, more active entities. The Zone is working harder.
- **Lachesis's "Signal Peak"** — the relay is transmitting. All players get a positional ping. The Zone is broadcasting everything, including you.
- **Ward 17's "Memory Cascade"** — a new storage node formed. A hidden room opens with rare loot. New data was encoded.
- **Threshold's "Boundary Test"** — the Zone edge shifted. A new path opens or closes. The Zone is probing its limits.

Each event makes the Zone feel like a living system with its own agenda, not a static backdrop with random modifiers.
