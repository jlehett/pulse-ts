# Shells

Shells are **printed biological bodies** that Runners deploy into Zones. All shells share identical base stats — same HP, same armor interaction, same movement speed, same inventory size, no card restrictions. Differentiation comes entirely from each shell's **passive ability**, **active ability**, and the **cores** that modify them.

For the narrative context behind shells (biological printing, consciousness transfer, reprinting after death), see [Lore Overview — Shells](./lore-overview.md#shells--printed-operators).

## Design Principles

- **All shells have identical base stats.** In a straight 1v1 fight with no abilities used, all shells are equal. Combat outcomes are determined by cards and decisions.
- **Each shell has one passive and one active ability.** These create a unique gameplay verb — something only that shell can do.
- **No card restrictions.** Any card works in any shell. Inventory size is universal.
- **Every shell works solo, in teams, with PvP, and without PvP.** No shell should feel like dead weight in any scenario.
- **Cores customize abilities, not stats.** Cores change *how* abilities function, not your numbers.

## Catalyst — "The one that weaponizes the Zone."

### Passive — Volatile

Catalyst's shell runs on a destabilized printing process — its stabilization layer is intentionally reactive. When Catalyst takes damage from any source (combat, PvE, Zone environmental effects), the impact destabilizes the shell's surface layer and releases a **Zone energy burst** on that node. The burst spikes the visibility of every other entity on the same node — they're revealed at maximum detection range for that turn.

Getting hit next to a Catalyst is like having a flare go off on top of you. PvE entities that hit the Catalyst accidentally expose themselves (and anything near them) to every Runner in range. Catalyst turns every fight it's in into a spectacle.

### Active — Destabilize

Target a node within 2 nodes. Catalyst remotely triggers a Zone energy reaction on that node: cover is reduced by one tier (Full→Partial, Partial→None) for several turns, and a visibility burst reveals everything on and adjacent to that node. Cooldown: several turns.

Destabilize a PoI room before entering — now you know what's inside and its cover is degraded. Destabilize a node an enemy is holding — strip their cover and reveal them to the map.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** Volatile punishes anything that fights you — PvE entities that attack reveal their whole area. Destabilize softens rooms before entering.
- **Team:** You're the breacher. Destabilize the room, team pushes in against degraded cover. Volatile means enemies who focus you expose themselves to the team.
- **Without PvP:** Volatile triggers from PvE damage, making every PvE encounter reveal the surrounding area. Destabilize strips cover from entrenched PvE positions.

### Lore

Catalyst prints use a stabilization process that UNZRA officially classifies as "non-compliant." The stabilization layer is over-saturated — packed with more Zone-reactive compounds than a shell needs. The excess energy has nowhere to go, so any impact on the shell triggers a cascade reaction in the surrounding Zone material.

Helix engineers stumbled on this during a botched printing run: a shell took a hit from a response entity and the entire corridor lit up like a signal flare. Every Zone system within 200 meters registered it. The engineer's report read: "Shell integrity held. Everything else in the room had a very bad day."

Catalyst Runners are walking disruption events. Other Runners don't love standing next to them. PvE entities don't love hitting them. The Zone itself seems to flinch when a Catalyst takes damage. The Wardens use Catalysts for demolition work — if you want a Zone process disrupted, put a Catalyst in front of it and wait for something to take a swing. Meridian considers them "environmentally hostile" and refuses to fund their printing.

Catalyst Runners tend to be unbothered by this. They tend to be unbothered by most things.

## Weaver — "The one that moves through walls."

### Passive — Residual Sense

The Zone isn't solid to a Weaver. The Imprint converted physical infrastructure into Zone material, but the original structure's layout still exists as a latent pattern inside the conversion — ghost architecture. Weaver's shell can read these **residual pathways**: connections that existed before the Imprint but aren't on the current node graph.

When Weaver visits a node, it can see **dormant connections** — old paths, sealed doors, collapsed corridors from the pre-Imprint structure. These appear as ghosted edges on Weaver's map. Not all are usable, but they reveal how the space used to flow.

### Active — Thread

Weaver activates a dormant connection, making it traversable for 1 turn — for the Weaver only. This lets Weaver move between two nodes that aren't connected on the normal graph (but were connected in the pre-Imprint layout). After the Weaver passes through, the connection goes dormant again.

Thread generates moderate visibility — the shell destabilizes Zone material as it passes through. You're not invisible while Threading, but you're *somewhere nobody expects*.

These paths are specific to each map, designed as part of the map's pre-Imprint history. Experienced Weaver players learn each map's hidden routes.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** You have access to routes nobody else does. Dead-end rooms aren't dead ends for you. PoIs with two entrances have five for you.
- **Team:** You're the flanker. Team pushes the front door; you Thread through a dormant corridor and arrive behind the defenders.
- **Without PvP:** Dormant connections bypass PvE chokepoints entirely. Response entities guard the main corridor — you go through the wall.

### Lore

The Imprint doesn't build from scratch. It converts. And conversion is imperfect — the original structure leaves an impression in the Zone material, like a fossil in rock. Most shells can't perceive these impressions. Weaver prints can.

The stabilization process used in Weaver shells incorporates a compound derived from Zone boundary material — the transitional substance found at the exact edge where normal matter meets Imprinted matter. This compound is sensitive to structural discontinuities. A Weaver standing in a converted hospital corridor can feel the sealed door to the left that used to lead to the pharmacy. The wall is solid Zone material now, but the boundary-compound in the Weaver's shell resonates with the discontinuity. The door is still there, latent, remembering what it was.

Threading is the act of forcing that memory to become real — briefly. The Weaver destabilizes the Zone material along the dormant connection and passes through before it re-solidifies. It's not teleportation. It's architecture that existed thirty years ago, momentarily asserting itself.

Edda researchers became obsessed with Weavers because the dormant connections are a map of the pre-Imprint world. Every path a Weaver finds is a piece of history. Some Weavers report that certain dormant connections feel *wrong* — paths that shouldn't have existed in the original structure. As if the Imprint didn't just convert what was there, but edited the history of what was there. Edda hasn't published these findings yet.

## Parasite — "The one that hijacks your senses."

### Passive — Bloodhound

Whenever any player or entity takes a combat action (firing, taking damage, killing) within 3 nodes of Parasite, Parasite detects their **exact node** — not a vague ping, the specific location. While other shells hear "gunfire somewhere nearby," Parasite knows exactly where the fight is, who's involved, and can infer what's happening. Parasite feeds on conflict.

### Active — Latch

Target a detected player, Shadow Player, or PvE entity within 2 nodes. For the next several turns, Parasite gains a **parasitic link**: you see everything the target sees. Their detection pings, the nodes they reveal, the loot they discover, the paths they take. You experience their information feed layered on top of your own.

The target receives a subtle indication — a UI flicker, a brief anomaly — that *something* happened. But they don't know what, who did it, or from where.

Latch on a Shadow Player is the sweet spot: Shadow Players are predictive models running optimized patrol routes through PoIs. Latching one gives you a free scout that's already systematically visiting loot nodes and assessing threats. Follow their feed, arrive after they've revealed everything, and take what you want.

Latch on a real player is espionage. You know their route, their destination, what cards they play. Ambush them at extraction, or simply avoid them entirely.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** Bloodhound tells you where fights are so you can steer toward or away. Latch turns Shadow Players into free scouts, making solo PoI runs much safer.
- **Team:** Latch on a threat gives the whole team intel on enemy movement. Bloodhound provides a combat-radar for the squad.
- **Without PvP:** PvE entities are valid Latch targets — latch a roaming patrol and you know its route. Bloodhound triggers from PvE combat too.

### Lore

The Parasite print is Vaunt's worst-kept secret. The shell's sensory architecture is wired to be *receptive* — it picks up Zone-frequency signals that other shells filter out as noise. Every combat action inside a Zone generates a signature in the Zone material: energy discharge, material stress, stabilization field interference. Most shells are calibrated to ignore this background radiation. Parasite shells are calibrated to *read* it.

Latch takes this further. The shell establishes a resonance pattern with a target's stabilization field — essentially tuning into the same frequency. The target's shell is constantly pinging its environment (all shells do this; it's how detection works), and Latch lets the Parasite receive those pings as if they were its own.

The subtle indication the target feels is real: Latch isn't perfectly clean. The resonance creates a minor feedback loop that manifests as a flicker in the target's sensory feed. Experienced Runners know what it means. "I've been Latched." But knowing you've been Latched and knowing *who* Latched you are very different things.

Parasite Runners have a reputation for being unsettling. They know things they shouldn't. They arrive at the right place at the right time. They watch you through someone else's eyes. UNZRA formally protests the Parasite print on ethical grounds (surveillance without consent). Vaunt formally doesn't care.

## Conductor — "The one that controls the Zone."

### Passive — Resonance

PvE entities within 1 node of Conductor are visible at all times (no detection check needed). Additionally, Conductor can read PvE entity **intent** — what action they're about to take next turn and where they're going to move. PvE behavior is transparent to Conductor.

### Active — Override

Target a PvE entity on Conductor's node. Spend one turn syncing with it. Once synced, Conductor can **direct that entity's behavior** for several turns: choose where it moves, when it attacks, what it targets. Conductor still acts normally on their own turn — the PvE entity is an additional piece on the board.

Override limits: only one entity at a time, the entity's base capabilities don't change (a maintenance drone can't suddenly fight like a response entity), and the sync breaks if Conductor takes damage or moves more than 3 nodes away. When Override ends, the entity resumes normal behavior.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** You never fight PvE alone — you turn the Zone's own systems against itself. Override a response entity at a PoI entrance and use it to clear rooms ahead of you.
- **Team:** PvE crowd control for the squad. Override the most dangerous entity and turn it into an ally.
- **Without PvP:** This is where Conductor *shines*. The entire PvE ecosystem is your toolkit. Every Zone entity is a potential ally, scout, or weapon.

### Lore

Meridian will deny developing the Conductor print. They'll say it was a collaborative effort with UNZRA, that the research was published openly, that the technology benefits everyone. This is technically true and functionally a lie.

The Conductor shell's stabilization process incorporates a signal-modulation compound that allows it to interface with Zone systems at the protocol level. PvE entities operate on instructions — material handling directives, patrol routes, threat response parameters — broadcast through the Zone's internal communication substrate. Conductor shells can *read* those instructions (Resonance) and, with enough proximity and synchronization time, *inject new ones* (Override).

The ethical implications are significant and largely unaddressed. PvE entities aren't sentient in any recognized sense — they're automated systems following directives. But they're automated systems built by the Architect, and overriding their directives is, in a meaningful sense, hacking alien infrastructure. The Wardens would love this technology if it weren't associated with Meridian. UNZRA publishes stern papers about it. Helix quietly contracts Conductor Runners for high-value extraction operations and doesn't talk about it publicly.

Conductor Runners describe the Override experience as "hearing the Zone's heartbeat and learning to change the rhythm." Some find it intuitive — the Zone's internal logic makes a kind of sense once you're immersed in it. Others find it alienating. A few long-career Conductors report that the Zone's instructions have started making more sense over time — not because the Conductors are getting better at reading them, but because the instructions are getting *simpler*. As if the Architect is optimizing its communication protocol. As if it's adapting to be understood.

## Phantom — "The one that's never where you think it is."

### Passive — Displacement

When a player or entity enters Phantom's node intending to interact (combat, etc.), Phantom can **displace** — instantly relocate to any adjacent node. This triggers after the enemy commits their action but before it resolves. The attacker arrives to find an empty room.

Displacement triggers automatically (Phantom can choose not to displace), has a cooldown of a couple turns, and generates no visibility. You just aren't there anymore.

This fundamentally changes how opponents approach a known Phantom. You can't just walk in and fight — you might be swinging at air. You need multiple players from multiple directions to cut off displacement routes.

### Active — Split

Phantom creates a **duplicate** on an adjacent node. Both the original and the duplicate appear identical on all detection systems. Both are "real" until the start of Phantom's next turn, when Phantom chooses which body to keep. The other vanishes.

During the split turn: if an enemy attacks the duplicate, they waste their combat action and reveal their position. If an enemy attacks the real one, normal combat occurs but Phantom can still Displacement-dodge. If nobody interacts with either, Phantom effectively scouted two directions at once.

Split + Displacement: put your duplicate on a dangerous node and your real body on a safe one. Enemy attacks the duplicate — wasted action. Or Split toward two PoI entrances, see what's in both, and choose which body to keep based on what you learned.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** Displacement means you're incredibly hard to kill. Split lets you scout safely. The safest solo shell.
- **Team:** You're the bait. Split toward a threat, draw their action to the duplicate, team pushes while they're committed.
- **Without PvP:** Displacement dodges PvE attacks — response entities commit to your node, you're gone. Split scouts PoI rooms without committing to enter.

### Lore

The Phantom print is the result of a UNZRA research accident that nobody has been able to fully explain.

During a stabilization experiment, a shell's bio-signature became *unstable* — it began fluctuating between two resonance states, effectively registering as present in two adjacent locations simultaneously. The shell wasn't actually in two places; its stabilization field was oscillating fast enough to create measurement uncertainty in Zone detection systems. The researcher's notes describe it as "quantum superposition, but for the Zone's sensors rather than for physics."

Displacement exploits this instability deliberately. When a threat enters the Phantom's node, the shell forces a rapid stabilization oscillation that collapses its presence on the current node and re-establishes it on an adjacent one. To the Zone's systems (and any Runner's detection), the Phantom simply stops being in one place and starts being in another. No movement, no transition — just a measurement that resolves differently.

Split pushes this further: the shell's stabilization field is deliberately split across two nodes, creating genuine measurement ambiguity. For the duration of the split, even the Zone itself cannot determine which body is "real." The Phantom chooses on the next turn by collapsing the field to one location.

Phantom Runners are rare. The printing process has a failure rate significantly higher than other shell types — the deliberately unstable stabilization sometimes doesn't stabilize at all, producing a non-functional shell. Successful Phantom Runners describe their experience as "being slightly out of phase with everything." They see the Zone differently — edges are softer, boundaries are suggestions, and the concept of "being somewhere" feels negotiable.

Other Runners find Phantoms unsettling. Not because they're dangerous (they are), but because they don't seem fully *committed* to existing in any particular location.

## Revenant — "The one that doesn't stay dead."

### Passive — Dead Sprint

When Revenant takes lethal damage, its shell doesn't shut down immediately. The stabilization compounds saturating the shell's biology continue firing for a brief window after biological death. Revenant enters **Dead Sprint** for 2 turns: it can MOVE but cannot fight, loot, use its active ability, or interact. After 2 turns, the shell collapses.

If Revenant reaches an extraction point during Dead Sprint, it extracts normally with everything it was carrying.

Dead Sprint follows normal movement rules — 1 node per MOVE action. No teleporting, no shortcuts. If extraction is 3 nodes away when you die, Dead Sprint isn't enough. Positioning matters.

Additionally, when a Revenant's shell finally collapses, its dropped cards are **degraded** — other players who loot the corpse get fewer usable items. The shell's death cascade corrupts the stabilization on the cards it was carrying. Killing a Revenant for its gear is a bad trade.

### Active — Mark

Tag a visible player or PvE entity. The Marked target is **tracked on Revenant's map at all times** — exact node, regardless of distance, terrain, or visibility — for the rest of the raid or until the target extracts/dies. Limited stock per raid (2–3 Marks).

Mark persists through Dead Sprint. You can track your killer while running — you know exactly where they are, which means you know which direction is *away from them*.

Mark also works offensively: tag a player entering your PoI and you always know where they are. Tag a roaming PvE entity and its patrol route is permanently visible.

### Scenario Viability

- **Solo:** Dead Sprint means aggressive play is less punishing — 2 extra turns to reach extraction. Mark tracks the biggest threat so you can route around it.
- **Team:** You're the point player. Push first, take the first hit, Dead Sprint back with intel while they push forward. Mark gives permanent tracking on priority targets.
- **Without PvP:** Mark on PvE entities gives permanent awareness of threats. Dead Sprint means failed PvE encounters aren't instant death.

### Lore

The first Revenant shell was an accident. Early in the printing program, a Runner named Kov took a lethal hit from a response entity inside a Synthesis Zone. The shell should have shut down. It didn't. The stabilization compounds in the shell's biology had saturated so deeply into the motor systems that they kept firing after the brain stopped sending signals — a chemical echo of the last conscious intention. Kov's last thought was "run." The shell ran.

Four hundred meters through a collapsing corridor, past two response entities that didn't register it as a threat (a dead shell reads as inert Zone material to maintenance systems), and out through the extraction seam. The haul Kov was carrying made it out intact. Kov's consciousness, safely stored at the printing facility, was loaded into a new shell the next day.

When they reviewed the extraction footage, the research team watched a clinically dead body sprint at full speed through an alien corridor for thirty-seven seconds. The room was quiet for a long time after the video ended.

UNZRA tried to replicate the phenomenon deliberately. It took two years and forty-one failed prints. The breakthrough was intentionally over-saturating the motor and sensory pathways with a specific stabilization compound ratio — enough that the shell's locomotion systems operate on chemical momentum for a brief window after biological death.

The window is short. Two turns. Maybe three with aggressive compound ratios, but that causes cascade degradation in everything the shell is carrying — the same chemicals keeping your legs moving are eating your gear.

Revenant Runners are a specific breed. They push harder than anyone else because they know death isn't the end of the run — it's the start of the last sprint. The community calls them "corpse runners." Revenant Runners call everyone else "people who stop too early."

There's a tradition among Revenant Runners: after a successful Dead Sprint extraction, you keep one card from the haul and mark it. Veterans have collections of "dead cards" — gear extracted by a body that was already dead. Some say it's superstition. Revenants say it's a record.

## Cores

Cores are **shell-specific mod slots** that modify how a shell's abilities function. They don't change stats or cards — they change the *behavior* of passive and active abilities.

Each shell has a **core slot**. Before a raid, you equip one core. Cores are persistent collection items — not consumed on use, not lost on death.

### How Cores Work

A core modifies one or both of a shell's abilities. The modification changes the ability's *function* in a meaningful way, creating sub-builds within a shell.

### Example Cores

**Catalyst:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Battering Ram** | Destabilize can now strip Full cover directly to None (bypasses the one-tier-at-a-time limit). |
| **Aftershock** | Volatile's burst also affects entities on nodes *adjacent* to Catalyst's node (reduced effect). Wider disruption. |
| **Ironclad** | Destabilize no longer generates a visibility burst, but the cover reduction lasts longer. Silent breaching. |

**Weaver:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Open Door** | Thread connections stay open for 2 turns, allowing allies (or enemies) to follow. |
| **Ghost Step** | Thread generates no visibility. Silent passage through walls. |
| **Cartographer** | Residual Sense detects dormant connections from 2 nodes away instead of requiring direct visit. |

**Parasite:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Pickpocket** | While Latched, Parasite siphons a small amount of loot value from the target's next looting action. |
| **Deep Link** | Latch duration is doubled but the target receives no anomaly indication. Completely silent. |
| **Web** | Can Latch two targets simultaneously but with shorter duration on each. |

**Conductor:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Remote Sync** | Override can be initiated from 1 node away instead of requiring same-node contact. |
| **Hivemind** | Can Override two entities simultaneously but with shorter duration. |
| **Mule** | Overridden entity can carry one loot item. Repurposed as a transport drone. |

**Phantom:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Long Step** | Displacement carries Phantom 2 nodes instead of 1. Deeper escape. |
| **Persistence** | Split duplicate persists for 2 turns instead of 1, extending the deception window. |
| **Mirror** | The duplicate generates the same detection signature as the real Phantom taking a combat action. It looks like it's fighting. |

**Revenant:**

| Core | Modification |
|---|---|
| **Afterimage** | During Dead Sprint, Revenant leaves a false contact at each node it passes through, lasting 1 turn each. Trail of ghosts. |
| **Vengeance** | Mark can be applied during Dead Sprint, and Marked targets are revealed to *all players in the raid*. Your killer becomes everyone's target. |
| **Second Wind** | Dead Sprint lasts 3 turns instead of 2, but card degradation on the Revenant's inventory begins immediately (some cards lost even if extraction succeeds). More time, higher cost. |

### Core Acquisition

Cores enter the collection through:

- **Raid loot** — Common cores drop from PoI loot tables. Rarer cores are in deeper nodes or specific Zones.
- **Faction rewards** — Each faction's upgrade tree includes cores as milestone unlocks. Faction-specific cores lean into that faction's playstyle.
- **Priority contract completions** — Seasonal quest chain completions reward unique cores not available elsewhere.
- **Crafting/trading** — TBD, but cores should be tradeable/craftable so players can work toward specific builds.

Cores are **not lost on death**. They're permanent collection items, like shells.
